Position Statement

"Engineering design pertains to the methodical and iterative progression of ideation, fabrication, and evaluation of a product to satisfy predetermined criteria" as per Arena Solutions [1]. If we deconstruct the concept, I believe that "engineering" mirrors the rigorous iterative process with extensive verification and testing to improve possible solutions. Then, "design" refers to a set of goals and constraints, like parameters that define a function() to return an output and address an opportunity successfully. Personally, I want to continue engaging in this kind of work, since there are countless aspects in society that I believe could be improved to optimize our health and well-being: engineering design is thus one way for me to contribute to the world.

Although my position on this topic continues to evolve, currently, I have solidified what the process looks like to me in Fig 1. Stemming from my programming experience from ESC180 and ESC190, I created a metaphoric diagram that represents my approach using the Python programming language. This is because I found that engineering design is closely related to computers that take a systematic, but extensive and resilient approach to running tasks to produce a final output.

Reflecting my student engineering experience so far, I believe that engineering design resembles a while loop: design iterations are continually executed and repeated so long as the goals are not satisfied or the evaluation compared to other designs deems the solution insufficient. Through my written perspective on the three prior design projects, they embody the reflection displayed in Fig 1, showing a repeated cycle of prototyping bridges, testing, evaluating, and refining until requirements are met where goals_satisfied() == True, and based on the evaluation criteria design_judgment() == "sufficient" to choose the best design.

Simultaneously, considering how most engineering projects are executed as a team, I find it vital to embrace four key values to remain on the same page and to produce the optimal final product. Noticeably, these values are similar to constant variables that remain immutable during a program's execution and support a function when called, as displayed in black in Fig 1.

  • "communication": Ensures a team is on the same page to avoid inconsistencies and unintended repetition of ideas.
  • "collaboration": Integrates diverse perspectives from other sources and team members to further improve solutions or generate new ideas.
  • "accountability": Reinforces responsibility in accomplishing the engineering design process, which could be essential in improving lives and society.
  • "resilience": Sustains progress despite setbacks, to learn from failures and avoid repeating the same mistakes.

To sum Fig 1, my approach to engineering design is to begin with an input() to identify the opportunity. Subsequently, we shall frame the problem with constraints, similar to defining function arguments so that a program does not run aimlessly. After undergoing an extensive design iteration loop, we can ultimately output a final design and present it to stakeholders. However, each function() or step in engineering design depends on the constants = ["communication", "collaboration", "accountability", "resilience"] to execute effectively. Similar to Python, all aspects in engineering design must be well-defined and well-structured, and only through many iterations can a team produce the best results.

Before, I did not consider the significant impact that frame() had, but now I have realized that it creates a basis which substantiates the reason for why a design team is aiming to achieve a goal in the first place. Then, within the while-loop structure, I found that engineering design processes including diverge(), converge(), refine(), etc. are all necessary to optimize the ultimate outcome. Accordingly, I present the specifics in my portfolio design projects through the use of nine distinct CTMFs. Nonetheless, my Python-inspired model of engineering design is one that represents resilience in the iterative process. Based on my experiences so far, engineering design is not a simple checklist of tasks, but a proactive loop of countless prototypes, tests, and improvements in order to address an opportunity.

Praxis I

Summary + Annotations

In Praxis I, my team finalized a design report communicating the best solution in response to the opportunity: removing liquid before recycling disposable cups, at the University of Toronto St. George (UTSG) Campus. The constants defined in my position statement were especially important to ensure team cohesion as well as developing the most optimal design as possible. Correspondingly, as displayed in Fig 2 and 3, we recommended a Vacuum Cleaning Device, a user-operated machine using air pressure to clean disposable cups, preventing liquid contamination in the trash, as we learned that even small amounts of liquid residue cannot be handled by recycling plants.

Before choosing our best design, we went through a rigorous diverging process including classical brainstorming, using SCAMPER and a morphological chart to explore the functionalities that need to implemented to fulfill the environmental problem. Then, we combined the functionalities to generate five (n + 1) concepts in aid for the convergence stage, aiming to prevent the issue of recycling contamination. However, in the interest of time, our convergence through a pairwise comparison, measurement matrix, and Pugh chart were mostly based on secondary research, which could have introduced selective bias in choosing the credible sources. Moreover, this research-based convergence led us to concluding that the vacuum design was the best prototype, and I now believe that this result was because the others seemed merely like proof of concepts, and the vacuum design was the easiest to implement its functionalities as per Fig 2 and 3. In terms of trade-offs, the primary verification and proxy tests to confirm the fit of this design was only on the vacuum design rather than the other four concepts, because we had no functioning prototype for them. Therefore, with more time in the future, I believe that the verification process should be equally rigorous among each prototype, to ensure that the proxy tests are consistently performed under the same conditions, rather than relying on a mixture of tests and secondary research.

Application: A 2024 waste audit of UTSG found that 44,240.71 coffee cups were put into dedicated recycling bins, but 0 were recycled. Diverge Classical Brainstorm

Definition: Before judging or converging down to a central solution, classical brainstorming is a diverging method used to generate as many ideas as possible without following contraints or a specific framework.

Application: To initialize the while-loop, we began brainstorming ideas on a chart paper to through discussions in tutorial sessions as well as basing our potential solution on the root cause analysis. Correspondingly, the classical brainstorm allowed us to further diverging using other tools, such as SCAMPER and Morph Charts, and get more into the specific functionalities of how a concept should remove the residual liquid from cups.

Future Implications: This experience suggests that future projects should treat brainstorming tools flexibly and prioritize idea quantity and openness when a team feels stuck too early in divergence.

Converge Measurement Matrix

Definition: A structured comparison tool that evaluates design alternatives against selected criteria to support decision-making.

Application: The formation of the measurement matrix was a rigorous process. For each prototype, there were respective proxy tests to quantify how well they surpass the evaluation criteria. All in all, the measurement matrix aided our final convergence to justify that a Vacuum Cleaning Device is the best design within the constraints of the opportunity.

Future Implications: Going forward, measurement matrix can continue supporting convergence by making trade-offs visible through a table that quantifies why one option best satisfies the opportunity and its constraints. Nevertheless, there were many biases during the process that may have influenced the final convergence. For instance, the weighting of the measurement matrix was based on a pairwise comparison in which our team created through an discussion to determine whether one option would perform better for stakeholders and why. The issue is that we are not scientists or environmentalists so it would of been more beneficial to form a basis with the evaluation criteria around CRAAP tested research sources rather than personal biases.

CIV102 Bridge Project

Summary + Annotations

The CIV102 bridge project involved designing, modeling, and constructing a matboard beam bridge capable of sustaining high loads to compete with other Engineering Science teams. The process including many iterations of computational analysis using Python, and physical prototyping to improve the bridge strength of a given "Design 0" by the course instructor. Key improvements included increasing cross-sectional height, adding diaphragms to prevent buckling, and reinforcing splice connections. The final design balanced structural optimization through a high second moment of area while meeting the constraints of the assignment, ultimately achieving sustained load of 720N in the bridge competition and winning second place out of 75 teams.

This project embodies the while-loop from my position statement both literally and figuratively, where Python code and repeated testing through prototyping led to a significantly improved final bridge from "Design 0". Namely, my bridge team iterated through several simulations with varying bridge dimensions through Python code, and we constructed two matboard bridge prototypes that underwent testing and evaluation. As proven from the result we had in the end, what made our team more successful was the fact that we had a first and second prototype. Although our team thus took more time constructing two bridges, we were able to identify any flaws in the first bridge's design and improve accordingly in the second iteration. Moving forward, with more time and materials, the while-loop design process can be run for longer with more verification and bridge strength testing to further optimize the bridge. As evidenced in this design project, increased prototyping and testing only benefits the ultimate solution. With that, I found that this continuous process is analogous to debugging code as per my position statement, which makes Python a comparable metaphor to engineering design as per the CIV102 bridge project.

Converge Proxy Test

Definition: A simplification of rigorous testing to assess alternative solutions' viability and feasibility through the retrieved data, without requiring access to advanced machinery.

Application: To optimize our matboard prototypes, we tested each one under real-world conditions to determine whether it failed or satisfied the basic requirements we had quantified. The proxy test exposed weaknesses at the splice and helped us identify where local failure controlled overall bridge behavior.

Future Implications: In future structural projects, proxy testing should be used earlier to reveal weak points before final fabrication so that design time is spent improving the most critical connections and load paths.

FDCR Perry Model

Definition: The Perry model of intellectual and ethical development encompasses four stages and respective transitions.

Application: Three stages of the Perry model best fit our engineering design process. (1) Initially, we began the divering process in the dualism stage, where I thought that varying the rectangular cross-sectional area was the only correct way of going about improving the sturdiness of the physical bridge. (2) However, realizing that this did not let us create ideas as flexibly as we thought, with suboptimal safety factor distribution results in Python, my team and I shifted our intellectual development to multiplicity. Namely, we found out through secondary research and first-hand Python testing, that manipulating the spacing of diaphragms is also one way to improve the safety factors as shown in Fig 11. This then gave rise to further creative solutions including an X-shaped diaphragm, a curved shape bridge, and more. (3) With this, I reached a stage of relativism, altogether recognizing that there is not always one correct approach in an engineering design process. For instance, I witnessed some other groups with a trapezoidal cross-section, a hollow triangular base, and more, elucidating the importance of open-mindedness in engineering design projects.

Future Implications: In future projects, this perspective encourages greater openness, stronger team morale, and more flexible decision-making because we are less likely to stay stuck on one rigid method when another approach may be more effective.

Robotic Arm Design

Summary + Annotations

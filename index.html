Position Statement

My metaphorical perspective on engineering design through Python code

"Engineering design pertains to the methodical and iterative progression of ideation, fabrication, and evaluation of a product to satisfy predetermined criteria" as per Arena Solutions [1]. To deconstruct the concept, I believe that "engineering" mirrors a rigorous iterative process with extensive verification and testing to improve solutions. Then, "design" refers to a set of goals and constraints, similar to parameters that define a function() to return an output and address an opportunity successfully. Personally, I aim to continue engaging in this kind of work, since there are countless design aspects in today's world that I believe could be improved to optimize our health and well-being: engineering design is thus one way for me to contribute to society.

Although my position on this topic continues to evolve, currently, I have solidified what the process looks like to me in Fig. 1. Stemming from my programming experience from ESC180 and ESC190, I created a metaphorical diagram that represents my approach using the Python programming language. This is because I found that engineering design is closely related to computers' way of taking a systematic, but extensive and resilient approach to running tasks to produce a final output.

Reflecting my student engineering experience so far, engineering design resembles a while loop: design iterations are continually executed and repeated so long as the goals are not satisfied or the evaluation compared to other designs deems the solution insufficient. Through my written perspective on the three prior design projects, they embody the diagram displayed in Fig. 1, showing a repeated cycle of prototyping bridges, testing, evaluating, and refining until requirements are met where goals_satisfied() == True, and based on the evaluation criteria design_judgment() == "sufficient" to choose the best design.

Simultaneously, considering how most engineering projects are executed as a team, I find it vital to embrace four key values to remain on the same page and to produce an optimal final product. Noticeably, these values are similar to constant variables that remain immutable during a program's execution and support a function when called, as displayed in black in Fig. 1.

  • "communication": Ensures a team is on the same page to avoid inconsistencies and unintended repetition of ideas.
  • "collaboration": Integrates diverse perspectives from other sources and team members to further improve solutions or generate new ideas.
  • "accountability": Reinforces responsibility in accomplishing the engineering design process, which could be essential in improving lives and society.
  • "resilience": Sustains progress despite setbacks, to learn from failures and avoid repeating the same mistakes.

To elucidate Fig. 1, my approach to engineering design is to begin with an input() to identify the opportunity. Subsequently, we shall frame the problem with constraints, similar to defining function arguments so that a program does not run aimlessly. After undergoing an extensive design iteration loop, we can ultimately output a final design and present it to stakeholders. However, each function() or step in engineering design depends on the constants = ["communication", "collaboration", "accountability", "resilience"] to execute effectively. Similar to Python, all aspects in engineering design must be well-defined and well-structured; only through many iterations can a team produce the best results.

Before, I did not consider the significant impact that frame() had, but now I have realized that it creates a basis which substantiates the reason for why a design team is aiming to achieve a goal in the first place. Then, within the while-loop structure, I found that engineering design methodologies including diverge(), converge(), refine(), etc. are all necessary to optimize the ultimate outcome. Accordingly, I present the specifics in my portfolio design projects through the use of twelve distinct CTMFs. Nonetheless, my Python-inspired model of engineering design is one that represents resilience in the iterative process. Based on my experiences so far, engineering design is not a simple checklist of tasks, but a proactive loop of countless prototypes, tests, and improvements in order to address an opportunity in the best possible way.

Praxis I

Designing a Device to Remove Liquid from Disposable Cups

In Praxis I, my team finalized a design report communicating the best solution in response to the input("Opportunity: ") removing liquid before recycling disposable cups, at the University of Toronto St. George (UTSG) Campus. The constants defined in my position statement were especially important to ensure team cohesion as well as in developing the most optimal design as possible. Correspondingly, as displayed in Fig. 2 and 3, we recommended a vacuum cleaning device, a user-operated machine using air pressure to clean disposable cups, preventing liquid contamination in the trash, as we learned that even small amounts of liquid residue cannot be handled by recycling plants.

Before choosing our best design, we went through a rigorous diverging cycle including classical brainstorming, using SCAMPER and a morphological chart to explore potential functions that concepts can possess to fulfill the environmental problem. Then, we combined the functions to generate n + 1 concepts in aid of the convergence stage, aiming to prevent recycling contamination. This included: a garbage bag filter, desiccant absorbers, a centrifuge, a cup compressor, and a vacuum cleaning device. However, in the interest of time, our convergence through a pairwise comparison, measurement matrix, and Pugh chart were mostly based on secondary research, which could have introduced selective bias in choosing the credible sources. Proceedingly, this research-based convergence led us to justify that the vacuum design was the best prototype. Looking back at this conclusion, I believe that this result was because the others seemed merely like proofs of concept, and the vacuum design was also the easiest to implement its functionalities with the materials we had, as per Fig. 2 and 3. With the lack of finished prototypes, to confirm the fit of this design, physical verification and proxy tests focused only on the vacuum design, where the other four concepts were verified through research. After this experiencing this convering process, I now feel that with more time in the future, the verification process should be instead be equally rigorous among each prototype, to ensure that proxy tests are consistently performed under the same conditions, rather than relying on a mixture of tests and secondary research.

Frame Root Cause Analysis

Definition: The identification of the underlying source of a problem rather than only addressing its visible symptoms.

Application: A 2024 waste audit of UTSG found that 44,240.71 coffee cups were put into dedicated recycling bins, but 0 were recycled. These cups made up 15.46% of the contamination in the recycling stream [2]. This confirms that the issue exists. More specifically, the root cause analysis provides my team and stakeholders with an understanding that it is not a lack of recycling infrastructure, but rather contamination caused by the residual liquids in disposable cups. Looking back, this framing ensured that our subsequent engineering design decisions were based on validated data, rather than assumptions. This ties to my position statement, since it sets a basis for the frame() before tackling the opportunity in the while-loop of the design project, and all executed decisions in the loop are to fulfill the framed goals set in the beginning. Adding on, this reflects my belief that there must be a well-defined input() so that our identification of the root cause can clearly guide our design decisions during the while-loop. Without this precisely framed understanding of the root cause, I believe that iterations can go on a tangent developing solutions to address the incorrect root cause of the problem.

Future Implications: As a student engineer, I was biased towards mechanical solutions when addressing problems through Praxis I, and also in Praxis II. In my current position, I recognize that there could be multiple approaches to tackling problems. For instance, rather than leaning towards a particular technological solution, I should be prioritizing solving the root cause of a problem that in fact has many aspects that we should consider including societal behaviours or habits that may influence users' way of disposing of cups.

Diverge Classical Brainstorm

Definition: Before judging or converging down to a central solution, classical brainstorming is a diverging method used to generate as many ideas as possible without following constraints or a specific framework.

Application: To initialize the while-loop, we began brainstorming ideas on a chart paper through discussions in tutorial sessions as well as basing our potential solution on the root cause analysis. Correspondingly, the classical brainstorm allowed us to further diverge using other tools, such as SCAMPER and Morph Charts, to visualize the specific functionalities of how a concept should remove the residual liquid from cups.

Future Implications: The brainstorming was followed by the two other diverging tools, however, the ideas were generated based on the team's bias focusing on solutions that remove or manage liquid as per the root cause analysis. Reflecting back to this experience, I recognized that there can be other contributing factors to the opportunity that tie directly with the root cause as aforementioned in the above section. To name a few, I recognize that different user behaviours or poor cup designs could have increased the residual liquid waste from cups. To thus stimulate a more divergent thinking and minimize biases, it would be beneficial to generate a wider range of concepts through more diverging tools, such as lotus blossom, wishing, biomimicry, etc.

Converge Measurement Matrix

Definition: A structured comparison tool that evaluates design alternatives against selected criteria to support decision-making.

Application: The formation of the measurement matrix in Fig. 5 was a rigorous process. For each prototype, there were respective proxy tests to quantify how well they surpass the evaluation criteria. All in all, the measurement matrix helped provide quantitative evidence towards the Pugh Chart and was central for our final convergence to justify that a vacuum cleaning device is the best design within the constraints of the opportunity.

Future Implications: Going forward, the continued use of measurement matrices is beneficial to support the converging cycle, by making constraints visible through a table that quantifies the extent to which one option satisfies an opportunity. Nevertheless, there were many biases during the process that may have influenced the final convergence. For instance, the weighting of the measurement matrix was based on a pairwise comparison in which my team created through discussion to determine whether one option would perform better for stakeholders and why. The issue is that we are not scientists or environmentalists so it would have been more beneficial to form a basis with the evaluation criteria around CRAAP tested research sources rather than personal biases.

CIV102 Bridge

Constructing a Matboard Bridge for the CIV102 Competition

The CIV102 bridge project involved designing, modeling, and constructing a matboard bridge beam capable of sustaining high loads to compete with other Engineering Science teams. The process included many iterations of computational analysis using Python, and physical prototyping to improve the bridge strength of a given "Design 0" by the course instructor. Key improvements included increasing cross-sectional height, adding diaphragms to prevent buckling, and reinforcing splice connections. The final design balanced structural optimization through a high second moment of area while meeting the constraints of the assignment, ultimately achieving a sustained load of 720N in the bridge competition and winning second place out of 75 teams.

This project embodies the while-loop from my position statement both literally and figuratively, where Python code and repeated testing through prototyping led to a significantly improved final bridge from "Design 0". Namely, my bridge team iterated through several simulations with varying bridge dimensions through Python code, and we constructed two matboard bridge prototypes that underwent testing and evaluation. Conversely to other teams, as demonstrated by the final results, my team's success was thanks to the fact we had a first and second prototype. Although we thus spent more time constructing two bridges, we were able to identify any flaws in the first bridge's design and improve accordingly in the second iteration. Moving forward, with more time and materials, the while-loop design process could be run for longer with more verification and bridge strength testing, which further optimizes the bridge as proven in this project. As evidenced in this design project, increased prototyping and testing only benefit the final solution. With that, I found that this iterative process is analogous to debugging code, which reinforces my position statement that makes Python a strong metaphor to engineering design as per the CIV102 bridge project.

(Thanks to my CIV102 Team: Junye, Terry and Navneet)

Converge Proxy Tests

Definition: A simplification of rigorous testing to assess alternative solutions' viability and feasibility through the retrieved data, without requiring access to advanced machinery.

Application: To optimize our matboard prototypes, we tested each one under simulated and real-world conditions to determine whether it failed or satisfied the basic requirements we had quantified. After the first bridge's dimensions passed all the simulated Python tests (the minimum loads required to pass the first stage of the competition), we then moved on to applying a load of approximately 400N at different locations of the bridge in real-world conditions. Contrasting with the results of the simulation, as illustrated in Fig. 9, there was a failure at the splice. This exposed one significant weakness of the bridge that we thought would not occur in initial Python calculations, which could have cost us in the actual bridge competition. Hence, I have recognized that proxy testing as part of evaluate() is vital, in the while-loop engineering design process, because it reveals the possible failure modes and material limitations that theoretical simulations may overlook. Simultaneously, my team was successful despite these failures, as we embraced constants[3] = "resilience" to sustain progress in making a successful bridge despite setbacks from proxy tests.

Future Implications: The CIV102 matboard bridge is a much smaller scale than a life-sized bridge for humans, however, smaller replicas like my team's design can still provide lots of information to civil engineers while using fewer materials. Accordingly, as seen with the possible failures that can occur during the first prototypes, it is essential to proxy test designs especially when human lives are at hand. Proxy tests are also useful to highlight hidden weaknesses of a design just like the splice strength of our bridge. Therefore, to optimize future designs, I will continue to proxy test as much as time permits to create the best engineering solutions.

Represent Prototype

Definition: A representation to generate and communicate information about the functionality of a design and aid the process of assessing alternatives.

Application: There were three stages that contributed to the prototyping process that I thought were vital. Firstly, OptiCutter was used to optimize our usage in cutting the matboard, which concretizes the importance of the converging stage as we had to verify whether our calculated bridge dimensions fit the constraints of the physical matboard. Secondly, we traced each bridge component in Fig. 10 to carefully verify the geometries a second time before performing irreversible cuts on the matboard. Eventually, we were confident in cutting and then gluing the matboard bridge together without concern for miscalculations. This highlights the fact that extensive verification before and during prototyping mitigates the room for errors, which is an aspect of engineering design that I should make a habit of as I continue my journey.

Future Implications: Looking back, I recognize that I was influenced by a form of anchoring bias, where I was fixated on replicating a prior successful bridge from upper years where they also varied diaphragm spacing and had non-uniform geometries to strengthen their bridge. However, this heavy reliance on using successful engineering designs as a reference does not guarantee success despite the fact that they performed well previously. This is because I eventually found out that our year had different constraints on how much load or how many train cars were going to run over the bridge compared to previous years. Therefore, I believe that I should develop my future designs relative to the current project conditions rather than completely relying on past successful designs.

FDCR Perry Model

Definition: The Perry model of intellectual and ethical development encompasses four stages and respective transitions.

Application: Three stages of the Perry model best fit our engineering design process. Initially, we began the diverging process in the dualism stage, where I thought that varying the cross-sectional shape was the only correct way of going about improving the sturdiness of the physical bridge. However, realizing through Python that this did not really improve the safety factor and strength results of our bridge, my team and I shifted our intellectual development to multiplicity. Namely, we found out through exploring past competition bridges, and Python simulations, that manipulating the spacing of diaphragms is also one way to improve the safety factors as shown in Fig. 13. This then gave rise to further creative solutions including an X-shaped diaphragm, a curved shape bridge, and more. With this, I reached a stage of relativism, ultimately recognizing that there is not always one correct approach in an engineering design process. For instance, I witnessed some other groups with a trapezoidal cross-section, a hollow triangular base, and more, elucidating the importance of open-mindedness in engineering design projects.

Future Implications: Currently, I am at "Transition 3: Joining values and analysis" from Fig. 12. I feel that I am transitioning from relativism to commitment within relativism where I am beginning to approach engineering design projects with an expectation that there are always multiple valid solutions that during the while-loop iterations where design_judgement == "sufficient", but simultaneously, if better information appears it is essential to continue to adapt and change the parameters to reframe(), rediverge(), or reconverge(). The CIV102 project had a glimpse of this ideology where we shifted our stance to variable dimensionalities to reinforce the bridge rather than using a linear cross-section and equal diaphragm spacing. Consequently, the mixture of commitment within relativism in future design projects would allow me to explore more opportunities outside the box to increase the chances of landing on an even more successful solution as proven with my CIV102 bridge.

Assembling a Functional and Dexterous Robotic Arm

I am a Design Engineer in the University of Toronto's Robotics for Space Exploration (RSX) arm subteam who collaborated with members to create a robotic arm with multiple degrees of freedom that is capable of lifting heavy loads, captured in Fig. 15. The design project is to ultimately assemble a functioning robotic arm, attach it to the rest of the rover that was made from other subteams, in order to enter the University Rover Challenge (URC) and compete against other universities. There continues to be yearly improvements on the robotic arm mechanics, embodying my engineering design interpretation of the while-loop. Frame PIAA

Definition: Our values and perspectives influence what we perceive. Accordingly, we must enhance our self-awareness and intentionality through following the decisions illustrated in Fig. 16: perceive (what you can see/hear/smell etc.), interpret (explore the possible meanings and apply engineering design models), assess (gather information to determine the most appropriate interpretation), and act accordingly [4].

Application: The University Rover Challenge (URC) provides a set of requirements for our robotic arm design that include performing several dexterous operations on a mock-up lander [5]. These are equipment servicing missions that form the basic requirements that the robotic arm must fulfill through proxy tests and verifications, which are fundamental to the engineering design approach. However, the greater the dexterity and the faster the arm is able to operate these tasks, the better. This manifests the PIAA model, which I noticed was very effective as reflected in my team's design process: after perceiving and interpreting the main goals, we transitioned to assessing the URC's guidelines by gathering information from our past robotic arms and then acted based on a plan that was well-coordinated as a team.

Future Implications: I interpret PIAA as a well-structured model that serves to create a plan. The plan across my two prior articulated design projects was not as structured, because there were time constraints and we often immediately delved into diverging, converging and prototyping without adequate planning and assessing before acting. Consequently, in my upcoming engineering design projects, I would focus on forming a plan parallel to the PIAA model, because this approach ensures decisions are intentional, and everyone remains aligned throughout the design process.

As shown from Fig. 17 to 18, the engineering design verifications represent continuous iterations until the FEA passes the requirements, likewise to while goals_satisfied() == False, which reinforces the accuracy of my metaphorical position statement as demonstrated through the prior two projects.

Future Implications: Throughout iterations in designing a robotic arm, I was heavily relying on FEA and simulations through SolidWorks. Prototyping a Gate Security System for Friends Daycare

To end things off, I will express my intepretation of engineering design through the Praxis II lens. To begin the process, among the given Request for Proposals (RFPs) from the teaching team, my team chose to input("Opportunity: ") to reduce uncertainty in child safety near daycare gates, to improve ease of supervision. With help from the RFP, we reframed and rescoped components such as needs, goals and objectives of stakeholders into a set of requirements and evaluation criteria, to make it clear for future design teams what to look out for when designing solutions to address the opportunity.

This proceedingly prompted the while-loop of my position statement. As elucidated in the Praxis I section we went through an extensive diverging and converging process. However, what made Praxis II different was that we took the while-loop in a more literal sense where we had a couple more rounds to rediverge() as we reframed the key requirements and evaluation criteria throughout iterations. Appropriately, because we diverged to new concepts, we were then provoked to reconverge() to new prototypes. Despite the fact that this process ultimately took significantly more time in comparison to my Praxis I diverging and converging process, this required us to repeatedly test(), evaluate() and refine() more rigorously, reflecting the essence of my position. This cycle at length allowed us to produce a "StepSafe Sentinel" prototype, which intricately measures an adult's weight that should be greater than all 2 to 5 year olds through a pressure plate, prompting them to press a button, to unlock the gate where otherwise, a flag raises immediately indicating to supervisors that a child is in an unauthorized location.

Frame Requirements

Definition: Stemming from stakeholders' values and expectations, and the opportunity, requirements are a set of metrics that a solution shall fulfill satisfy needs.

Application: Using the RFP that we have chosen, creating the set of requirements to address the opportunity was an ongoing rescoping process that began during frame() and continued to be modified during converge(). Firstly, we reframed the needs, goals and objectives into defined requirements based on what we justified to be primary stakeholders, which included the children and supervisors. The requirements were especially important during the convering process as this involved prototype verification to ensure all the requirements were met. However, as we continued to gather information on how existing mechanisms work and what Friends Daycare stakeholders aim to achieve in terms of supervision, some requirements were changed to address new knowledge. Ergo, while requirements in this way are flexible to modify, all of them must be fulfilled by successful prototypes in order to remain consistent to address all sides of the opportunity.

Future Implications: At times, the teaching team through assignments and tutorials would ask which of our requirements was the most important for this opportunity, and our team found it difficult to answer with a strong justification. This questioning process made me realize that a larger set of requirements does not necessarily mean effective requirements. My team had many requirements at the time, which led to prototypes that had to compromise certain functionalities in order to satisfy every other requirement. Therefore, moving forward, I will aim to prioritize a more concise set of requirements, because I would have a clearer direction during prototyping, to create more refined solutions by concentrating on fewer critical aspects at a time.

Diverge Morphological Chart

Definition: A diverging tool to break down complex problems into subfunctions and respectively list potential solutions through visualizations or sketches.

Application: The morphological chart was useful in the diverging process, because the solution had to address two key aspects of the opportunity: enhance supervision of children and prevent child gate access. Hence, the morphological chart was chosen as one of our primary diverging tools, sine it allowed us to split the problem into smaller subfunctions, which made it easier to diverge for ideas. This break down of concepts thus provided many permutations of prototypes that we mixed-and-matched to converge from dozens of prototypes, increasing the likelihood of identifying a stronger final solution as we narrowed them down one-by-one through verification and converging tools.

Future Implications: As indicated in the Praxis I section, I am already familiar with using the morphological chart. In future diverging stages, I should rather explore concepts through other divering tools, because relying too heavily on one method across all my design projects can introduce functional fixedness. This fixedness is a cognitive bias that limits a person's ability to problem-solve when they are only familiar with the same frameworks or approaches [7]. By repeatedly using similar tabulated tools, developmental psychology reveals that it only constrains outside-the-box thinking. Consequently, it would be beneficial to incorporate a broader range of diverging methods going forward, to potentially develop more novel or unconventional ideas, reinforcing my same argument about these implications in the Praxis I, Classical Brainstorm section.

FDCR Toulmin's Structure of Argument

Definition: A structure that consistents of core components to develop a strong argument as seen in Fig. 25: ground (starting point), claim (something we assert), justification (logical reasoning for the claim), evidence (facts and data to backup claim and justification), qualifier (limitations), and counterclaim (perspective on other views). Accordingly, it creates a loop to determine whether or not the claim should be accepted as true [6]

Application: Through Praxis II, written works such as a Request for Proposal and presentations: Beta Release and Showcase were strong demonstrations of how the Toulmin's structure of argument is crucially embedded throughout the engineering design process. In terms of the opportunity, the ground was established through gathering information about child safety concerns at daycare gates and the main claim that we wanted stakeholders and design teams to accept was that the "StepSafe Sentinel" is the best gate system to address the problem. Correspondingly, the claim was supported by justifications and evidence throughout the converging process as we went through verification methods, primary and secondary research, to backup the reason why this design is optimal. To also weigh in the weaknesses, we acknowledged qualifiers, including how weight-based detection systems may not always be reliable (in special cases adults weigh less than children). This recognition then allowed us to explore alternative solutions through counterclaims, which led to refine() our design by adding a component that the adult had to also press a button that no child can reach, in addition to stepping on the pressure plate. This continuous cycle of the Toulmin's structure mirrors my metaphorical while>-loop. Each iteration of evaluate() allowed my team to consider both the strengths and weaknesses, which helped us modify and strengthen the claim, as evident with our evolved prototype (from just a pressure plate and flag mechanism to a more robust system involve a pressure plate, button, and flag) to reliably guarantee that only adults can access gates, but also improving ease of supervision.

Future Implications: In my opinion, no matter the design project I take on in the future, I will continue to apply the Toulmin's structure of argument to develop credible engineering claims. In particular, in professional settings, I would need to master applying this structure to communicate evidence-based arguments, so that stakeholders can clearly understand my point of view on how and why a design works. As exemplified through the assignments in Praxis II, the teaching team reading or listening will always have juxtaposing questions to claims, in which we would had to back it up rigorously. This process is also reflected in the real-world, because there will always be stakeholders and other oppositions to my proposed design solution, in which the Toulmin's structure of argument would help me reinforce my credibility.

Position Statement

"Engineering design pertains to the methodical and iterative progression of ideation, fabrication, and evaluation of a product to satisfy predetermined criteria" as per Arena Solutions [1]. To deconstruct the concept, I believe that "engineering" mirrors a rigorous iterative process with extensive verification and testing to improve solutions. Then, "design" refers to a set of goals and constraints, similar to parameters that define a function() to return an output and address an opportunity successfully. Personally, I aim to continue engaging in this kind of work, since there are countless design aspects in today's world that I believe could be improved to optimize our health and well-being: engineering design is thus one way for me to contribute to society.

Although my position on this topic continues to evolve, currently, I have solidified what the process looks like to me in Fig. 1. Stemming from my programming experience from ESC180 and ESC190, I created a metaphorical diagram that represents my approach using the Python programming language. This is because I found that engineering design is closely related to computers' way of taking a systematic, but extensive and resilient approach to running tasks to produce a final output.

Reflecting my student engineering experience so far, engineering design resembles a while loop: design iterations are continually executed and repeated so long as the goals are not satisfied or the evaluation compared to other designs deems the solution insufficient. Through my written perspective on the three prior design projects, they embody the diagram displayed in Fig. 1, showing a repeated cycle of prototyping bridges, testing, evaluating, and refining until requirements are met where goals_satisfied() == True, and based on the evaluation criteria design_judgment() == "sufficient" to choose the best design.

Simultaneously, considering how most engineering projects are executed as a team, I find it vital to embrace four key values to remain on the same page and to produce an optimal final product. Noticeably, these values are similar to constant variables that remain immutable during a program's execution and support a function when called, as displayed in black in Fig. 1.

  • "communication": Ensures a team is on the same page to avoid inconsistencies and unintended repetition of ideas.
  • "collaboration": Integrates diverse perspectives from other sources and team members to further improve solutions or generate new ideas.
  • "accountability": Reinforces responsibility in accomplishing the engineering design process, which could be essential in improving lives and society.
  • "resilience": Sustains progress despite setbacks, to learn from failures and avoid repeating the same mistakes.

To elucidate Fig. 1, my approach to engineering design is to begin with an input() to identify the opportunity. Subsequently, we shall frame the problem with constraints, similar to defining function arguments so that a program does not run aimlessly. After undergoing an extensive design iteration loop, we can ultimately output a final design and present it to stakeholders. However, each function() or step in engineering design depends on the constants = ["communication", "collaboration", "accountability", "resilience"] to execute effectively. Similar to Python, all aspects in engineering design must be well-defined and well-structured; only through many iterations can a team produce the best results.

Before, I did not consider the significant impact that frame() had, but now I have realized that it creates a basis which substantiates the reason for why a design team is aiming to achieve a goal in the first place. Then, within the while-loop structure, I found that engineering design methodologies including diverge(), converge(), refine(), etc. are all necessary to optimize the ultimate outcome. Accordingly, I present the specifics in my portfolio design projects through the use of nine distinct CTMFs. Nonetheless, my Python-inspired model of engineering design is one that represents resilience in the iterative process. Based on my experiences so far, engineering design is not a simple checklist of tasks, but a proactive loop of countless prototypes, tests, and improvements in order to address an opportunity.

Praxis I

Designing a Device to Remove Liquid from Disposable Cups

In Praxis I, my team finalized a design report communicating the best solution in response to the opportunity: removing liquid before recycling disposable cups, at the University of Toronto St. George (UTSG) Campus. The constants defined in my position statement were especially important to ensure team cohesion as well as in developing the most optimal design as possible. Correspondingly, as displayed in Fig. 2 and 3, we recommended a vacuum cleaning device, a user-operated machine using air pressure to clean disposable cups, preventing liquid contamination in the trash, as we learned that even small amounts of liquid residue cannot be handled by recycling plants.

Before choosing our best design, we went through a rigorous diverging cycle including classical brainstorming, using SCAMPER and a morphological chart to explore potential functions that concepts can possess to fulfill the environmental problem. Then, we combined the functions to generate n + 1 concepts in aid of the convergence stage, aiming to prevent recycling contamination. This included: a garbage bag filter, desiccant absorbers, a centrifuge, a cup compressor, and a vacuum cleaning device. However, in the interest of time, our convergence through a pairwise comparison, measurement matrix, and Pugh chart were mostly based on secondary research, which could have introduced selective bias in choosing the credible sources. Moreover, this research-based convergence led us to justify that the vacuum design was the best prototype, and I now believe that this result was because the others seemed merely like proofs of concept, and the vacuum design was the easiest to implement its functionalities as per Fig. 2 and 3. To confirm the fit of this design, physical verification and proxy tests focused only on the vacuum design, where the other four prototypes were verified through research. Unfortunately, this outcome was due to the fact that we had no functioning prototype for the remaining designs. Therefore, with more time in the future, I believe that the verification process should be equally rigorous among each prototype, to ensure that the proxy tests are consistently performed under the same conditions, rather than relying on a mixture of tests and secondary research.

Frame Root Cause Analysis

Definition: The identification of the underlying source of a problem rather than only addressing its visible symptoms.

Application: A 2024 waste audit of UTSG found that 44,240.71 coffee cups were put into dedicated recycling bins, but 0 were recycled. These cups made up 15.46% of the contamination in the recycling stream [2]. This confirms that the issue exists. More specifically, the root cause analysis provides our team and stakeholders with an understanding that it is not a lack of recycling infrastructure, but rather contamination caused by the residual liquids in disposable cups. Looking back, this framing ensured that our subsequent engineering design decisions were based on validated data, rather than assumptions. This ties to my position statement, since it sets a basis for the frame() before tackling the opportunity in the while-loop of the design project, and all executed decisions in the loop are to fulfill the framed goals set in the beginning. Adding on, this reflects my belief that there must be a well-defined input() so that our identification of the root cause can clearly guide our design decisions during the while-loop. Without this precisely framed understanding of the root cause, I believe that iterations can go on a tangent developing solutions to address the incorrect root cause of the problem.

Future Implications: As a student engineer, I was biased towards mechanical solutions when addressing problems through Praxis I, and also in Praxis II. In my current position, I recognize that there could be multiple approaches to tackling problems. For instance, rather than leaning towards a particular technological solution, I should be prioritizing solving the root cause of a problem that in fact has many aspects that we should consider including societal behaviours or habits that may influence users' way of disposing of cups.

Diverge Classical Brainstorm

Definition: Before judging or converging down to a central solution, classical brainstorming is a diverging method used to generate as many ideas as possible without following constraints or a specific framework.

Application: To initialize the while-loop, we began brainstorming ideas on a chart paper through discussions in tutorial sessions as well as basing our potential solution on the root cause analysis. Correspondingly, the classical brainstorm allowed us to further diverge using other tools, such as SCAMPER and Morph Charts, to visualize the specific functionalities of how a concept should remove the residual liquid from cups.

Future Implications: The brainstorming was followed by the two other diverging tools, however, the ideas were generated based on the team's bias focusing on solutions that remove or manage liquid as per the root cause analysis. Reflecting back to this experience, I recognized that there can be other contributing factors to the opportunity that tie directly with the root cause as aforementioned in the above section. To name a few, I recognize that different user behaviours or poor cup designs could have increased the residual liquid waste from cups. To thus stimulate a more divergent thinking and minimize biases, it would be beneficial to generate a wider range of concepts through more diverging tools, such as lotus blossom, wishing, biomimicry, etc.

Converge Measurement Matrix

Definition: A structured comparison tool that evaluates design alternatives against selected criteria to support decision-making.

Application: The formation of the measurement matrix in Fig. 5 was a rigorous process. For each prototype, there were respective proxy tests to quantify how well they surpass the evaluation criteria. All in all, the measurement matrix helped provide quantitative evidence towards the Pugh Chart and was central for our final convergence to justify that a vacuum cleaning device is the best design within the constraints of the opportunity.

Future Implications: Going forward, the continued use of measurement matrices is beneficial to support the converging cycle, by making constraints visible through a table that quantifies the extent to which one option satisfies an opportunity. Nevertheless, there were many biases during the process that may have influenced the final convergence. For instance, the weighting of the measurement matrix was based on a pairwise comparison in which our team created through discussion to determine whether one option would perform better for stakeholders and why. The issue is that we are not scientists or environmentalists so it would have been more beneficial to form a basis with the evaluation criteria around CRAAP tested research sources rather than personal biases.

CIV102 Bridge

Constructing a Matboard Bridge for the CIV102 Competition

The CIV102 bridge project involved designing, modeling, and constructing a matboard bridge beam capable of sustaining high loads to compete with other Engineering Science teams. The process included many iterations of computational analysis using Python, and physical prototyping to improve the bridge strength of a given "Design 0" by the course instructor. Key improvements included increasing cross-sectional height, adding diaphragms to prevent buckling, and reinforcing splice connections. The final design balanced structural optimization through a high second moment of area while meeting the constraints of the assignment, ultimately achieving a sustained load of 720N in the bridge competition and winning second place out of 75 teams.

This project embodies the while-loop from my position statement both literally and figuratively, where Python code and repeated testing through prototyping led to a significantly improved final bridge from "Design 0". Namely, my bridge team iterated through several simulations with varying bridge dimensions through Python code, and we constructed two matboard bridge prototypes that underwent testing and evaluation. Conversely to other teams, as demonstrated by the final results, our team's success was thanks to the fact we had a first and second prototype. Although our team thus spent more time constructing two bridges, we were able to identify any flaws in the first bridge's design and improve accordingly in the second iteration. Moving forward, with more time and materials, the while-loop design process could be run for longer with more verification and bridge strength testing, which further optimizes the bridge as proven in this project. As evidenced in this design project, increased prototyping and testing only benefit the final solution. With that, I found that this iterative process is analogous to debugging code, which reinforces my position statement that makes Python a strong metaphor to engineering design as per the CIV102 bridge project.

Converge Proxy Tests

Definition: A simplification of rigorous testing to assess alternative solutions' viability and feasibility through the retrieved data, without requiring access to advanced machinery.

Application: To optimize our matboard prototypes, we tested each one under simulated and real-world conditions to determine whether it failed or satisfied the basic requirements we had quantified. After the first bridge's dimensions passed all the simulated Python tests (the minimum loads required to pass the first stage of the competition), we then moved on to applying a load of approximately 400N at different locations of the bridge in real-world conditions. Contrasting with the results of the simulation, as illustrated in Fig. 9, there was a failure at the splice. This exposed one significant weakness of the bridge that we thought would not occur in initial Python calculations, which could have cost us in the actual bridge competition. Hence, I have recognized that proxy testing as part of evaluate() is vital, in the while-loop engineering design process, because it reveals the possible failure modes and material limitations that theoretical simulations may overlook. Simultaneously, my team was successful despite these failures, as we embraced constants[3] = "resilience" to sustain progress in making a successful bridge despite setbacks from proxy tests.

Future Implications: The CIV102 matboard bridge is a much smaller scale than a life-sized bridge for humans, however, smaller replicas like my team's design can still provide lots of information to civil engineers while using fewer materials. Accordingly, as seen with the possible failures that can occur during the first prototypes, it is essential to proxy test designs especially when human lives are at hand. Proxy tests are also useful to highlight hidden weaknesses of a design just like the splice strength of our bridge. Therefore, to optimize future designs, I will continue to proxy test as much as time permits to create the best engineering solutions.

Represent Prototype

Definition: A representation to generate and communicate information about the functionality of a design and aid the process of assessing alternatives.

Application: There were three stages that contributed to the prototyping process that I thought were vital. Firstly, OptiCutter was used to optimize our usage in cutting the matboard, which concretizes the importance of the converging stage as we had to verify whether our calculated bridge dimensions fit the constraints of the physical matboard. Secondly, we traced each bridge component in Fig. 10 to carefully verify the geometries a second time before performing irreversible cuts on the matboard. Eventually, we were confident in cutting and then gluing the matboard bridge together without concern for miscalculations. This highlights the fact that extensive verification before and during prototyping mitigates the room for errors, which is an aspect of engineering design that I should make a habit of as I continue my journey.

Future Implications: Looking back, I recognize that I was influenced by a form of anchoring bias, where I was fixated on replicating a prior successful bridge from upper years where they also varied diaphragm spacing and had non-uniform geometries to strengthen their bridge. However, this heavy reliance on using successful engineering designs as a reference does not guarantee success despite the fact that they performed well previously. This is because I eventually found out that our year had different constraints on how much load or how many train cars were going to run over the bridge compared to previous years. Therefore, I believe that I should develop my future designs relative to the current project conditions rather than completely relying on past successful designs.

FDCR Perry Model

Definition: The Perry model of intellectual and ethical development encompasses four stages and respective transitions.

Application: Three stages of the Perry model best fit our engineering design process. Initially, we began the diverging process in the dualism stage, where I thought that varying the cross-sectional shape was the only correct way of going about improving the sturdiness of the physical bridge. However, realizing through Python that this did not really improve the safety factor and strength results of our bridge, my team and I shifted our intellectual development to multiplicity. Namely, we found out through exploring past competition bridges, and Python simulations, that manipulating the spacing of diaphragms is also one way to improve the safety factors as shown in Fig. 13. This then gave rise to further creative solutions including an X-shaped diaphragm, a curved shape bridge, and more. With this, I reached a stage of relativism, ultimately recognizing that there is not always one correct approach in an engineering design process. For instance, I witnessed some other groups with a trapezoidal cross-section, a hollow triangular base, and more, elucidating the importance of open-mindedness in engineering design projects.

Future Implications: Currently, I am at "Transition 3: Joining values and analysis" from Fig. 12. I feel that I am transitioning from relativism to commitment within relativism where I am beginning to approach engineering design projects with an expectation that there are always multiple valid solutions that during the while-loop iterations where design_judgement == "sufficient", but simultaneously, if better information appears it is essential to continue to adapt and change the parameters to reframe(), rediverge(), or reconverge(). The CIV102 project had a glimpse of this ideology where we shifted our stance to variable dimensionalities to reinforce the bridge rather than using a linear cross-section and equal diaphragm spacing. Consequently, the mixture of commitment within relativism in future design projects would allow me to explore more opportunities outside the box to increase the chances of landing on an even more successful solution as proven with my CIV102 bridge.

Robotic Arm

Assembling a Functional and Dexterous Robotic Arm

I am a Design Engineer in the University of Toronto's Robotics for Space Exploration (RSX) arm subteam who collaborated with members to create a robotic arm with multiple degrees of freedom that is capable of lifting heavy loads. To ultimately assemble the final design, I focused on prototyping individual parts with SolidWorks, to ensure they fulfill proxy testing requirements before integrating components together. To name a few parts, I CAD'd: a SparkMax cantilever mount, a finger casing design, a sheet metal junction of two gearboxes, and more.

There continues to be yearly improvements on the robotic arm mechanics, embodying my engineering design interpretation of the while-loop. As the requirements for robotic competitions change every year and with newer advanced materials, the team must go through continuous iterations to improve upon prior designs to construct another successful robotic arm. Furthermore, I noticed that constants[0:2] = ["communication", "collaboration"] from my position statement are especially important for a large design team like RSX. This is because throughout these two semesters, even when not meeting in-person, RSX subteam leaders communicate updates and assign tasks on platforms like Discord and through email to maintain coordination with a large number of members.

Frame PIAA

Definition: Our values and perspectives influence what we perceive. Accordingly, we must enhance our self-awareness and intentionality through following the decisions illustrated in Fig. 15: Perceive (what you can see/hear/smell etc.), interpret (explore the possible meanings and apply engineering design models), assess (gather information to determine the most appropriate interpretation), and act accordingly [4].

Application: The University Rover Challenge (URC) provides a set of requirements for our robotic arm design that include performing several dexterous operations on a mock-up lander [5]. These are equipment servicing missions that form the basic requirements that the robotic arm must fulfill through proxy tests and verifications, which are fundamental to the engineering design approach. However, the greater the dexterity and the faster the arm is able to operate these tasks, the better. This manifests the PIAA model, which I noticed was very effective as reflected in my team's design process: after perceiving and interpreting the main goals, we transitioned to assessing the URC's guidelines by gathering information from our past robotic arms and then acted based on a plan that was well-coordinated as a team.

Future Implications: I interpret PIAA as a well-structured model that serves to create a plan. The plan across my two prior articulated design projects was not as structured, because there were time constraints and we often immediately delved into diverging, converging and prototyping without adequate planning and assessing before acting. Consequently, in my upcoming engineering design projects, I would focus on forming a plan parallel to the PIAA model, because this approach ensures decisions are intentional, and everyone remains aligned throughout the design process.

Converge Verification

Definition: A process of checking whether a design satisfies the intended specifications, dimensions, and performance requirements.

Application: The verification happened alongside the CAD'ing process in order to identify any issues before ultimately converging on specific design dimensions. Besides, verifying CAD models helped us minimize wasting materials that can come with 3D-printing unverified parts. As seen in Fig. 16, there are regions in the metal that do not pass a simple yield strength test using Finite Element Analysis (FEA), a method to predict how parts react to real-world forces. If this component would have been assembled with the robotic arm, the outcome would have been detrimental when applying a certain torques on the robotic arm according to the URC competition feats. Therefore, I found that the FEA verification process in engineering design was crucial, because it allowed me to visualize and redimension my robotic arm parts accordingly to make it stronger and adhere to URC guidelines as shown in Fig. 17. As shown from Fig. 16 to 17, the engineering design verifications represent continuous iterations until the FEA passes the requirements, likewise to while goals_satisfied() == False, which reinforces the accuracy of my metaphorical position statement as demonstrated through the prior two projects.

Future Implications: Throughout iterations in designing a robotic arm, I was heavily relying on FEA and simulations through SolidWorks. However, as suggested by the team lead, I have noted that iterating upon past years' designs like Fig. 18, which could act as a reference that I could aim to improve on in the future. This is relevant since it could serve as another form of verification that I could use in the future, where I could design prototypes that are better than the already qualified past designs.

Represent CAD

Application: Allows computer-aided simulations to help enhance the verification process of how much the arm component can withstand forces and torques, before 3D printing.
